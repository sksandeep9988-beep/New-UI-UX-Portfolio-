01 — Overview

What is StockMood?

StockMood is a stock market research and portfolio management web application designed for modern investors. The platform aggregates market data, financial analysis, news sentiment, ownership data, and personal portfolio tracking into a single, cohesive dashboard experience.

The design spans 9 key screens — from a high-level market overview to granular research tools covering financial statements, news analysis, institutional ownership, and portfolio performance — all tied together with a consistent, data-forward visual language.

02 — Problem & Solution

The Gap in Existing Platforms

Most retail investors juggle multiple tools to research stocks — one platform for charts, another for financials, a third for news, and a spreadsheet for their portfolio. This fragmented experience leads to poor decisions, missed signals, and information overload. StockMood was designed to solve exactly this.

⚠ The Problem

  • Data is scattered across Bloomberg, Yahoo Finance, Reddit, and spreadsheets
  • Existing dashboards are visually cluttered with no hierarchy
  • Retail investors lack access to institutional-grade research tools
  • No unified view of portfolio performance alongside market context
  • News and sentiment are disconnected from price data
  • Ownership and insider activity is hidden in complex filings

✅ The Solution

  • Single dashboard combining market overview + deep research + portfolio
  • Clean data hierarchy: summary → detail → action
  • Research tabs (Financial, News, Analysis, Ownership) per stock
  • Personal portfolio tracker with visual performance metrics
  • Mood score indicator gives sentiment at a glance
  • Institutional ownership and key metrics in one view
03 — Research & Target Users

Who Uses StockMood?

Understanding the user base was critical because finance products have distinctly different user segments — each with different mental models, urgency levels, and data literacy. Three primary personas were identified:

📈

Retail Investor

The Portfolio Builder

Monitors 10–30 stocks, checks performance weekly, needs clear portfolio visualization and simple research summaries without jargon.

🔬

Stock Analyst

The Deep Researcher

Analyzes fundamentals — income statements, balance sheets, DCF models. Needs fast access to financial tabs and historical data comparison.

⚡

Active Trader

The Market Watcher

Reacts to news, sentiment, and price momentum. Needs real-time mood scores, trending stocks, and fast-loading charts with minimal friction.

Visual Language

07 — Key UX Decisions

Why These Choices Were Made

🎯

Tabbed Research Architecture

Instead of one overwhelming stock page, research was split into 5 tabs (Overview, Financial, News, Analysis, Ownership). This respects the user's mental model — you don't need financial data and news simultaneously. Each tab serves a specific decision-making context.

📊

Green/Red as Universal Financial Language

Teal anchors the brand identity throughout UI chrome, while pure green and red are reserved exclusively for financial gain/loss signals. This creates a clear semantic separation — users never confuse a brand color with a market signal.

🌡

Market Mood Score — Single Number Clarity

The Mood Score (e.g., 0.78 / 1.240) distills complex market sentiment into a single, scannable number prominently displayed in the Overview. This gives even casual users an instant market temperature check without reading through tables.

🗂

Consistent Left Sidebar Navigation

Every screen uses an identical left sidebar layout. This anchors users spatially — regardless of how deep into research tabs they go, the nav structure never changes. Reducing navigation anxiety is critical in complex data products.

📰

Dense Tables with Color-Coded Rows

Financial data is inherently tabular. Instead of trying to "visualize away" the tables, the design embraces them with clean typography, subtle row striping, and color-coded value cells. This respects analyst-level users who need density without sacrificing readability for newcomers.

🖥

Two Overview Layout Variants

Two versions of the Market Overview page (v1 and v2) were designed — one emphasizing the mood score and charts prominently, the other prioritizing the stock table and news feed. This allows for A/B testing to identify which layout better serves primary user actions upon landing.

09 — Learnings & Reflections

What This Project Taught Me

Data Density is a Design Feature

For finance products, showing more data is often the right choice. Hiding information behind toggles frustrates power users. The challenge is organizing density, not reducing it.

Color Carries Meaning — Protect It

Using green and red only for financial signals (never for decorative purposes) ensures users never misread a UI element as a market movement. Semantic color discipline matters more here than in any other domain.

Navigation Stability Reduces Anxiety

Keeping the sidebar consistent across all 9 screens — even though the content complexity varies enormously — made the prototype feel like one product, not a collection of dashboards.

The "Mood Score" Concept

Abstracting market sentiment into a single number was the most impactful UX decision. It gives casual users immediate value and gives power users a quick sanity check — serving both personas with one design element.

"

Good finance UX doesn't simplify data — it simplifies the path to understanding it. The designer's job is to make complexity navigable, not invisible.

— Design Philosophy, StockMood Project
10 — Conclusion

Final Thoughts

StockMood is more than a dashboard — it's an attempt to democratize institutional-quality stock research for the modern retail investor. Every design decision, from the tabbed research architecture to the semantic color system, was made to serve a specific user need in a specific investment context.

The result is a 9-screen, high-fidelity Figma prototype that covers the full investment workflow — from morning market check to deep stock research to portfolio review. The design is scalable, component-based, and ready for developer handoff.

Next Steps: Usability testing with retail investors and analysts, mobile-responsive adaptation, dark mode variant (especially relevant for active traders), and integration of real-time data API specifications for engineering handoff.
