01 — Overview

Project at a Glance

This project involved designing a full-featured, multi-page website for a logistics and transport company that provides road freight, air cargo, sea freight, and warehousing services. The goal was to create a digital presence that communicates professionalism, builds client trust, and makes it easy for businesses to explore services and get in touch.

The design spans four key pages — Home, Services, About Us, and Contact — each strategically crafted to guide visitors through the company's offerings and ultimately convert them into leads.

02 — Problem & Solution

What Problem Does This Solve?

Most logistics companies in the market either have outdated websites with poor navigation, or generic templates that fail to communicate their specific service offerings and value proposition. Clients — especially B2B decision-makers — need to quickly understand what is offered, whether the company is trustworthy, and how to get a quote or make contact.

⚠ The Problem

  • No clear visual hierarchy on existing logistics sites
  • Services are buried under confusing menus
  • No trust signals (fleet size, years of experience, coverage)
  • Weak CTAs — users don't know what to do next
  • No dedicated About or team section — reduces credibility
  • Contact forms are hard to find or too complex

✅ The Solution

  • Bold hero section with clear value proposition
  • Dedicated Services page with detailed breakdowns
  • Stats, heritage, and global reach sections for trust
  • Prominent orange CTA buttons throughout all pages
  • Dedicated About Us page with leadership & mission
  • Clean, minimal contact page with form + map
03 — Research & Insights

Understanding the Target Audience

The primary users of this website are B2B clients — procurement managers, supply chain heads, and business owners looking for reliable logistics partners. Secondary users include fleet managers and cargo handlers seeking route and capacity information. Research involved competitor analysis of logistics websites and review of user behavior patterns in the industry.

73%

B2B buyers research online before contacting a vendor

8 sec

Average time to judge website credibility at first glance

68%

Users leave if services or pricing are hard to find

Key Research Findings

Users in the logistics space prioritize three things above all: speed (can I find what I need quickly?), trust (does this company look established and reliable?), and clarity (are the services clearly explained?). These three pillars directly shaped the design decisions throughout this project.

Competitor analysis revealed that most logistics websites use either a plain corporate look with no personality, or overcrowded layouts with too much information per screen. The opportunity was to balance professional credibility with a clean, modern UI that guides users naturally from awareness to conversion.

05 — Page Breakdown

Page-by-Page Design Decisions

Each page has a specific role in the user journey — from awareness and education to trust-building and conversion. Here's how each page was approached:

1

Home Page

The homepage acts as the primary landing experience. It leads with a bold hero section featuring a truck visual, a strong headline, and dual CTAs. Below the fold, sections build credibility progressively — "About Us," "Why Choose Us," "How We Work," "Our Services," and "Our Fleet" — ending with a footer that includes a quick quote form.

Key Sections:

Hero + CTA About snippet Why Us (icons) How We Work Services overview Fleet gallery Quick Quote form Footer
2

Services Page

This page dives deep into each service category — road freight, air cargo, sea freight, local transport, and warehousing. Each service is presented with real imagery, a brief description, key metrics (capacity, transit time, coverage), and a CTA button. Comparison tables make it easy to evaluate options.

Key Sections:

Road Freight Air Cargo Sea Freight Local Transport Warehousing Service tables CTA banners
3

About Us Page

The About page humanizes the brand. It features a mission/vision statement, a "Our Heritage" section with founding story and stats, a global presence map, leadership team profiles, and a "The Mindful Architecture" editorial section — ending with a compelling "Ready to move at the speed of Kinetic?" CTA.

Key Sections:

Mission statement Heritage & stats Global map Leadership team Values editorial Bottom CTA
4

Contact Page

The Contact page is designed for frictionless lead capture. A "Contact Our Global Support" header anchors the dark hero section. Below it, a clean contact form sits alongside office location info. A world map visual reinforces the company's global reach, and a "Our Services" quick-reference is included for last-minute clarity before the user submits.

Key Sections:

Dark hero header Contact form Location details World map Services reference
06 — Key UX Decisions

Why These Design Choices?

1

Dark Navy + Orange Color Scheme

Navy conveys strength, reliability, and professionalism — qualities critical for a B2B logistics brand. Orange is used exclusively for CTAs and key data, creating a strong visual hierarchy that guides the user's eye without overwhelming them. This pairing also sets the brand apart from the typical "blue corporate" look of competitors.

2

Hero Section with Dual CTAs

Two buttons — "Get a Quote" (primary) and "Our Services" (secondary) — cater to users at different stages. Some visitors are ready to act; others need to explore first. The dual CTA respects this difference without creating friction for either group.

3

Stats & Numbers as Trust Signals

Numbers like "Years in Business," "Routes Covered," "Fleet Size," and "Deliveries Completed" appear across the Home and About pages. These are not decorative — they serve as quantified social proof, reducing hesitation in B2B buyers who need evidence before choosing a logistics partner.

4

Real Images for Fleet & Services

Instead of generic icons or illustrations, real truck and cargo images are used throughout the Services and Fleet sections. This communicates authenticity and gives the design a documentary credibility — showing rather than telling. It also helps users visualize the actual scale and quality of operations.

5

Structured "How We Work" Section

A step-by-step process diagram demystifies how the company operates — from inquiry to delivery. This is a significant trust-builder because it shows transparency and systematic professionalism, something B2B clients value heavily when choosing a long-term logistics partner.

6

Quick Quote Form in Footer

Placing a lightweight quote form in the footer ensures that even users who scroll to the very end — without clicking any CTA — still have an immediate conversion path available. This captures intent at the point when the user is most informed and most likely to act.

08 — Learnings & Reflections

What I Learned

B2B Design Requires Restraint

Unlike B2C design where emotion drives decisions, B2B buyers respond to clarity, credibility, and efficiency. Every element must earn its place on the screen by serving one of these three functions.

Color as a Functional Tool

Limiting orange to CTAs, labels, and key stats transformed the color from a decoration into a functional guide — users intuitively know what to click without being told.

Page Flow Is a User Journey

Each page must answer a specific question the user has at that stage. Home = "What do you do?" Services = "Can you handle my needs?" About = "Can I trust you?" Contact = "How do I start?"

Consistency Builds Trust

Repeated use of the same component patterns, icon styles, and spacing across all four pages created a sense of organizational structure — making the company feel systematic and dependable.

09 — Conclusion

Final Thoughts

This logistics website design demonstrates how thoughtful UI/UX choices can turn a functional website into a strategic business tool. Every section, every color choice, every CTA placement was made with a clear purpose — to guide a business decision-maker from first impression to confident action.

The design is ready for developer handoff via Figma, with a well-structured prototype that clearly communicates the intended user journey across all four pages. The next steps would include usability testing with real logistics clients, accessibility audit (WCAG 2.1 AA compliance), and responsive adaptation for tablet and mobile viewports.
